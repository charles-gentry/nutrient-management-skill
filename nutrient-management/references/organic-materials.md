## 1. Principles of Nitrogen Supply and Losses

Nitrogen is present in organic materials in two main forms:
- **Readily available nitrogen** (ammonium-N, nitrate-N and uric acid-N) — potentially available for rapid crop uptake
- **Organic-N** — broken down slowly to become available over months to years

**Crop-available nitrogen** = readily available N remaining after losses + N released from organic forms.

### Ammonia Volatilisation
- Typically around 40% of readily available N is lost following surface application
- Rapid incorporation reduces losses: within 6 hours for liquids, 24 hours for solids on tillage land
- Band-spreading and shallow-injection reduce ammonia emissions by 30–70% compared to broadcast
- Losses are higher from high DM materials, dry soils, and warm conditions

### Nitrate Leaching
- Mainly related to soil type, application rate, readily available N content, and rainfall after application
- Autumn/early-winter applications should be avoided — overwinter rainfall washes nitrate out before crop uptake
- Delaying to late winter/spring reduces leaching and increases N utilisation efficiency

### Release of Crop-Available N from Organic Materials
- Organic-N is released (mineralised) slowly over months to years
- Around 5% of total N may become available for the second crop following application
- N not taken up may be lost by overwinter leaching or accumulate in soil organic matter

## 2. Phosphate, Potash and Magnesium

- Availability of manure phosphate to next crop: typically **50–60%** (lower than water-soluble P fertilisers)
- Around **90%** of manure potash is readily available for crop uptake
- Where crop responses to P or K are expected (soil Index 0 or 1), use the **available** (not total) P and K content
- At target Index (usually Index 2) or above, use the **total** P and K content in nutrient balance-sheet calculations
- At soil P Index 3+, ensure total phosphate inputs do not exceed crop offtake during the rotation

## 4. Using Organic Materials and Fertilisers Together

Follow this 6-step process:

1. **Calculate** the quantity of crop-available nutrients supplied by each application of organic material
2. **Identify** fields that will benefit most (high N demand crops first; low P/K Index fields benefit more; target silage fields over grazing-only)
3. **Plan** application rates for each field, managing phosphate over the rotation. Apply in late winter to summer for best N use
4. **Aim** for organic material to supply no more than 50–60% of total crop N requirement; use manufactured fertiliser for the balance
5. **Calculate** the crop nutrient requirement, then deduct nutrients from organic materials to give the manufactured fertiliser balance
6. **Calibrate** application equipment for the type of material being applied

### Application Timing
- Apply from late winter to summer to maximise nitrogen value and minimise nitrate leaching
- Materials with high readily available N (slurry, poultry manure, digestate) are subject to NVZ closed-spreading periods
- Rapid soil incorporation on tillage land (within 6 hours for liquids) minimises ammonia losses
- On grassland, do not graze for at least 4 weeks after application or until all visible signs have disappeared

## 9. Biosolids

Biosolids (treated sewage sludge) are valuable fertilisers and soil conditioners. Applications must comply with the Sludge (Use in Agriculture) Regulations 1989, the Code of Practice for Agricultural Use of Sewage Sludge, and the Biosolids Assurance Scheme (BAS).

Key points:
- Most applications supply more phosphate than the following crop removes — manage over the rotation
- Biosolids contain only small amounts of potash but useful quantities of sulphur and magnesium
- Lime-stabilised biosolids have neutralising value typically 2–6% per tonne fresh weight
- Biosolids contain heavy metals — statutory requirement to analyse topsoil for metals before spreading
- Soil sampling every 3–5 years is essential on fields receiving biosolids

## 10. Compost

Compost is produced by the controlled biological decomposition of green waste or green/food waste in the presence of oxygen. It is both a soil conditioner and nutrient source. Repeated use increases soil organic matter, improving workability and water retention.

Key points:
- PAS100-certified compost does not normally need an environmental permit for land application
- Green compost supplies negligible crop-available nitrogen — do not reduce N fertiliser rates
- Green/food compost: ~5% of total N is available to the next crop (irrespective of timing)
- Around 50% of compost phosphate is available to next crop
- Around 80% of compost potash is readily available

## 11. Digestate

Digestate is a product of anaerobic digestion of biodegradable materials (food wastes, animal manures, crops). Also known as 'biofertiliser' if PAS110 certified.

Key points:
- Whole digestate: ~5% DM, slurry-like. Can be separated into liquid and fibre fractions
- 80–90% of N in whole/liquid digestate is readily available — subject to NVZ closed-spreading periods
- Separated fibre (~25% DM): usually <30% of total N is readily available — generally not subject to closed periods
- Food-based digestates typically have much higher nutrient contents than farm-sourced
- Ammonia emissions from digestate can be greater than from cattle slurries — use precision-application equipment
- Around 60% of digestate phosphate and 90% of potash is available to the next crop

| Biologically treated | 30 | 7.5 | 3.8 | 0.4 | 2.4 | 1.0 |

Following application of chemically/physically treated paper crumble, nitrogen 'lock-up' commonly occurs due to high C:N ratio. Around 0.8 kg of inorganic N per tonne (FW) is required to compensate. Biologically treated paper crumble has a lower C:N ratio and lock-up is not usually experienced.
